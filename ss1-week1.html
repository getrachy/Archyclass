Week 1: Introduction to Technical Drawing

First Term | SS1 Technical Drawing

What is Technical Drawing?

Technical drawing, also called drafting or engineering drawing, is a form of visual communication used to clearly and accurately represent objects, structures, or systems. It uses standardized symbols, lines, and conventions to convey information that can be understood universally by engineers, architects, designers, and technicians.

Types of Technical Drawing

  • Orthographic Drawing – Shows an object from multiple views (front, top, side)
  • Isometric Drawing – A 3D representation on a 2D plane at 30° angles
  • Perspective Drawing – Mimics how the human eye sees objects (used in architecture)
  • Oblique Drawing – Front face drawn true shape, sides drawn at an angle
  • Sectional Drawing – Shows the inside of an object by cutting through it
  • Assembly Drawing – Shows how parts fit together
  • Detail Drawing – Shows a single component in full detail
  • Schematic Drawing – Uses symbols to show systems (electrical, plumbing)

Advantages of Technical Drawing

  • Universal language understood across countries
  • Provides precise measurements and specifications
  • Eliminates guesswork in construction and manufacturing
  • Serves as a legal document in contracts
  • Helps detect errors before production begins

Disadvantages

  • Requires specialized training and skill
  • Time-consuming when done manually
  • Errors in drawing can lead to costly mistakes
  • Manual drawings can be damaged or lost

Why TD is the Language of Architecture

Before any building in Lagos is constructed, it exists first as a technical drawing. Architects use TD to communicate exact dimensions to builders. Without TD, the Lekki Bridge, National Theatre, or your house would collapse from guesswork.
