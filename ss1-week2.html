1. Introduction to Technical Drawing

What is Technical Drawing?

Technical drawing (also called drafting or engineering drawing) is a form of visual communication used to clearly and accurately represent objects, structures, or systems. It uses standardized symbols, lines, and conventions to convey information that can be understood universally by engineers, architects, designers, and technicians.

Types of Technical Drawing

  • Orthographic Drawing – Shows an object from multiple views (front, top, side)
  • Isometric Drawing – A 3D representation on a 2D plane at 30° angles
  • Perspective Drawing – Mimics how the human eye sees objects (used in architecture)
  • Oblique Drawing – Front face drawn true shape, sides drawn at an angle
  • Sectional Drawing – Shows the inside of an object by cutting through it
  • Assembly Drawing – Shows how parts fit together
  • Detail Drawing – Shows a single component in full detail
  • Schematic Drawing – Uses symbols to show systems (electrical, plumbing)

Advantages

  • Universal language understood across countries
  • Provides precise measurements and specifications
  • Eliminates guesswork in construction and manufacturing
  • Serves as a legal document in contracts
  • Helps detect errors before production begins

Disadvantages

  • Requires specialized training and skill
  • Time-consuming when done manually
  • Errors in drawing can lead to costly mistakes
  • Manual drawings can be damaged or lost

2. Importance of Technical Drawing in Engineering & Architecture

Overview

Technical drawing is the backbone of both engineering and architecture. It bridges the gap between an idea and its physical realization.

In Engineering

  • Used to design machines, components, and systems
  • Helps in manufacturing with exact dimensions
  • Used for quality control and inspection
  • Essential for patents and intellectual property documentation

In Architecture

  • Used to plan buildings, roads, bridges
  • Communicates design intent to builders and clients
  • Required for planning permissions and approvals
  • Helps in cost estimation and material planning

3. Drawing Instruments and Materials

Overview

These are the tools and materials used to produce accurate and neat technical drawings.

Types of Drawing Instruments

A. Measuring & Drawing Tools

  • T-Square – Used to draw horizontal lines and as a guide for set squares
  • Set Squares (45° and 30°/60°) – Used to draw angles and vertical lines
  • Ruler/Scale Rule – For measuring and drawing straight lines
  • Protractor – For measuring and drawing angles
  • Compass – For drawing circles and arcs
  • Divider – For transferring measurements and dividing lines equally
  • French Curves – For drawing irregular curves
  • Flexible Curve – Bendable tool for custom curves

B. Drawing Surfaces

  • Drawing Board – Flat surface to place drawing sheets
  • Drawing Table – Adjustable surface for professional use

C. Writing/Marking Instruments

  • Pencils (H, HB, B grades) – Different hardness for different line weights
  • Technical Pens/Rapidograph – For ink drawings with consistent line width
  • Eraser – For corrections
  • Erasing Shield – Protects surrounding lines when erasing

D. Drawing Materials

  • Drawing Paper/Cartridge Paper – Standard paper for pencil drawings
  • Tracing Paper – Transparent paper for copying drawings
  • Drawing Film/Vellum – Durable, professional drafting material
  • Drawing Pins/Clips – For securing paper to the board
  • Masking Tape – Holds paper without damaging it

4. Care and Proper Use of Drawing Instruments

Overview

Proper care of drawing instruments ensures their longevity, accuracy, and the quality of work produced.

Care of Specific Instruments

Compass

  • Keep the needle point sharp
  • Store in a case to avoid bending
  • Do not force the joints — adjust gently
  • Replace leads when worn

Pencils

  • Sharpen regularly to a fine conical point
  • Use a sandpaper block to maintain the point
  • Different grades for different line types

T-Square & Set Squares

  • Keep edges clean — wipe with a damp cloth
  • Store flat to prevent warping
  • Never use as a cutting guide (damages edges)
  • Check edges for nicks regularly

Drawing Board

  • Keep the surface clean and smooth
  • Never cut on the drawing board
  • Store vertically to prevent warping

Technical Pens

  • Clean after use to prevent ink clogging
  • Store horizontally or cap tightly
  • Use the correct ink type

French Curves & Templates

  • Clean ink/graphite after use
  • Store flat to prevent bending

General Rules of Care

  1. Always store instruments in a proper case or box
  2. Keep all instruments dry — moisture causes rust and damage
  3. Never drop instruments, especially compasses and pens
  4. Clean instruments before and after use
  5. Handle with clean hands to avoid oil transfer to paper

Advantages of Proper Care

  • Instruments last longer
  • Drawings are more accurate
  • Saves money on replacements
  • Maintains professional quality

Disadvantages of Poor Care

  • Inaccurate drawings
  • Damaged instruments
  • Increased costs
  • Poor-quality output

5. Drawing Sheet Layout (Borders, Title Block, Margin)

Overview

A drawing sheet layout is the organized arrangement of a technical drawing on paper, following standardized conventions to ensure clarity and professionalism.

Standard Paper Sizes (ISO)

Components of a Drawing Sheet Layout

A. Margin

  • The space between the edge of the paper and the border
  • Typical margin: 10–20mm on most sides, 20–25mm on the binding side (left)
  • Protects the drawing from damage at the edges

B. Border

  • A rectangle drawn inside the margin using a thick continuous line
  • Defines the drawing area
  • Standard border line thickness: 0.7mm – 1.0mm

C. Title Block

Located at the bottom right corner of the sheet. Contains:

  • Drawing Title – Name of the object/project
  • Drawing Number – Unique reference number
  • Scale – The scale used (e.g., 1:10)
  • Date – When the drawing was made
  • Drawn By – Name of the drafter
  • Checked By – Name of the reviewer
  • Sheet Number – e.g., Sheet 1 of 3
  • Company/Institution Name and Logo
  • Projection Symbol – 1st or 3rd angle projection

D. Revision Block

  • Records changes made to the drawing
  • Includes revision number, description, date, and initials

E. Folding Marks

  • Small marks to guide folding the sheet to A4 size for filing

Advantages of Standard Layout

  • Easy to identify and file drawings
  • Provides all necessary information at a glance
  • Maintains professionalism
  • Required for official and legal submissions

Disadvantages

  • Takes time to set up manually
  • Mistakes in the title block can cause confusion
  • Not all organizations use the same format

Detailed Descriptions

Outline/Object Line (Thick Continuous)

  • Heaviest line on a drawing
  • Defines the visible shape of an object
  • Always drawn first

Dimension Line (Thin Continuous)

  • Has arrowheads at both ends
  • Shows the measurement of a feature
  • Never breaks for a dimension figure in modern standards

Extension/Projection Line (Thin Continuous)

  • Extends from the object to the dimension line
  • Does not touch the object directly (small gap left)

Centre Line (Chain Thin)

  • Alternating long and short dashes
  • Marks the centre of circles, arcs, and symmetrical features
  • Extends slightly beyond the feature

Hidden Detail Line (Dashed)

  • Short, equally spaced dashes
  • Shows edges and outlines that are not visible from the viewing direction

Section/Cutting Plane Line

  • Shows where an imaginary cut is made to reveal interior details
  • Has arrows showing the direction of view

Advantages of Standardized Lines

  • Universal understanding across different countries/firms
  • Reduces confusion and misinterpretation
  • Makes drawings cleaner and more readable

Disadvantages

  • Requires practice to master all line types
  • Poor line quality makes drawings unreadable
  • Manual consistency is difficult to achieve

7. Lettering – Vertical Single Stroke

Overview

Lettering in technical drawing refers to the writing of text, numbers, and annotations on drawings. Vertical single stroke lettering uses upright (90°) characters formed with single strokes of a pencil or pen.

Characteristics

  • Letters are perfectly vertical (90° to baseline)
  • Each stroke is made in one direction — no retracing
  • Height-to-width ratio is standardized
  • Style is clear, simple, and legible

Standards (ISO 3098)

FeatureSpecification
Uppercase height7mm (standard)
Lowercase height5mm
Stroke width1/10 of letter height
Spacing between letters2mm

Types of Vertical Lettering

  • Uppercase (Capital) Letters – Used for titles, headings
  • Lowercase Letters – Used for notes and descriptions
  • Numerals – Used for dimensions and scales
