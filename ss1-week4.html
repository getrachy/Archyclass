📐 SS1 Technical Drawing: Week 4
Lettering – Vertical & Inclined (Gothic)

Learning Objectives: By the end of this lesson, students should be able to construct vertical single-stroke letters and inclined Gothic letters according to ISO standards.

1. Lettering – Vertical Single Stroke

Overview

Lettering in technical drawing refers to the writing of text, numbers, and annotations on drawings. Vertical single stroke lettering uses upright (90°) characters formed with single strokes of a pencil or pen.

Characteristics

  • Letters are perfectly vertical (90° to baseline)
  • Each stroke is made in one direction — no retracing
  • Height-to-width ratio is standardized
  • Style is clear, simple, and legible

Standards (ISO 3098)

FeatureSpecification
Uppercase height7mm (standard)
Lowercase height5mm
Stroke width1/10 of letter height
Spacing between letters2mm
Spacing between words6mm
Spacing between lines10mm

Types of Vertical Lettering

  • Uppercase (Capital) Letters – Used for titles, headings
  • Lowercase Letters – Used for notes and descriptions
  • Numerals – Used for dimensions and scales
VERTICAL UPPERCASE EXAMPLE
ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ
1234567890

How to Practice

  1. Draw guidelines using thin lines (cap line, waist line, base line, drop line)
  2. Practice each letter individually
  3. Maintain consistent height and spacing
  4. Use an HB or H pencil

Advantages

  • Easy to read at any angle
  • Looks clean and professional
  • Suitable for all types of technical drawings
  • Universally accepted standard

Disadvantages

  • Requires consistent practice
  • Difficult to master uniform spacing
  • Time-consuming for beginners

2. Lettering – Inclined (Gothic Style)

Overview

Inclined lettering is similar to vertical lettering but the characters are slanted at 68° to 75° (commonly 68°) to the horizontal. Gothic style refers to the simple, uniform stroke-width letterforms used in technical drawing — not decorative Gothic script.

Characteristics

  • Letters lean to the right at approximately 68°
  • Single stroke construction
  • Uniform stroke width throughout
  • No serifs (clean, simple endings)

Comparison: Vertical vs Inclined

FeatureVerticalInclined (Gothic)
Angle90°68°–75°
AppearanceUprightSlanted right
UseGeneral drawingsSome engineering drawings
ReadabilityHighHigh
DifficultyModerateSlightly harder

How to Draw Inclined Letters

  1. Draw the same guidelines as vertical lettering
  2. Add guide lines at 68° using a protractor or special template
  3. All vertical strokes follow the 68° angle
  4. Curved letters are elliptical, not circular
INCLINED GOTHIC EXAMPLE
ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ
1234567890

Advantages

  • Gives a dynamic, modern look to drawings
  • Easy to distinguish from vertical lettering
  • Widely used in American engineering standards (ANSI)
  • Adds variety and visual interest

Disadvantages

  • Harder to maintain consistent angle
  • Less common in ISO standards
  • Requires more practice than vertical lettering
  • Can look untidy if angle is inconsistent
Assignment: On an A4 sheet, write the alphabet A-Z and numbers 0-9 using:
1. Vertical single stroke (7mm height)
2. Inclined Gothic style (7mm height, 68° slant)
Use proper guidelines and spacing.
