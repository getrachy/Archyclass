📐 SS1 Technical Drawing: Week 7
Lettering (Types, Sizes, Guidelines)

Learning Objectives: By the end of this lesson, students should be able to produce clear, uniform letters and numbers according to ISO standards for technical drawings.

1. Overview

Lettering is the art of writing letters and numbers on a technical drawing. It is used for titles, dimensions, notes, and labels.

Unlike artistic handwriting, technical lettering must be legible, uniform, and standardized so that anyone can read the drawing without confusion.

Poor lettering can ruin an otherwise perfect drawing. WAEC and NECO mark lettering heavily.

2. Types of Lettering

A. Single-Stroke Lettering

  • Most common in technical drawing
  • Each line of a letter is made with one stroke of the pencil or pen
  • Simple, fast, and easy to read
  • Can be Vertical or Inclined/Sloping (75° to the right)
VERTICAL: ABCDEFG
INCLINED: ABCDEFG

B. Gothic Lettering

  • Bold, heavy strokes
  • Used mainly for main titles and headings
  • Takes longer to produce than single-stroke

C. Roman Lettering

  • Has serifs (small decorative strokes at ends of letters)
  • Used in architectural drawings for formal titles
  • Not common in engineering drawings

3. Standard Letter Sizes (ISO)

Letter height is called the "nominal size". The standard progression is based on √2 ratio:

Size (mm)UsePen Size
2.5Dimension figures, notes0.25mm
3.5Sub-titles, section letters0.35mm
5.0Main titles, drawing numbers0.5mm
7.0Major headings0.7mm
10.0Large posters, cover sheets1.0mm

SS1 Standard: Use 3.5mm for general notes and 5.0mm for titles on A4 sheets.

4. Guidelines for Lettering

A. Drawing Guidelines

  1. Always use light horizontal guidelines (4H pencil) before lettering
  2. Draw top, bottom, and middle guidelines for capitals and lower case
  3. Guidelines ensure uniform height and alignment
  4. Erase guidelines lightly after inking

B. Spacing Rules

  • Between letters: 1/5 of letter height (e.g., 1mm gap for 5mm letters)
  • Between words: Equal to letter height (e.g., 5mm gap for 5mm letters)
  • Between lines: 3/5 of letter height minimum
  • Visual spacing: Adjust for letter shapes - "LA" needs less space than "HH"

C. Lettering Technique

  1. Use HB or F pencil for pencil lettering. Keep point sharp.
  2. Draw vertical strokes downwards – Better control
  3. Draw horizontal strokes left to right
  4. Draw curved strokes in one motion – Don't stop halfway
  5. Keep letters uniform – Same height, width, and slant
  6. Use CAPITALS only – Lower case is not used in technical drawings
  7. Leave space between letters – Don't let letters touch

5. What to Letter on a Drawing

  • Title of drawing – Largest text, 5.0mm minimum
  • Dimensions – 2.5mm or 3.5mm, placed above dimension line
  • Notes – 3.5mm, for specifications and instructions
  • Part numbers – For assembly drawings
  • Scale, date, drawn by – In title block, 2.5mm or 3.5mm
  • Section labels – A-A, B-B in 5.0mm

6. Advantages of Good Lettering

  • Improves clarity – Drawing can be understood quickly
  • Looks professional – Essential for passing exams and industry work
  • Prevents misinterpretation – Clear dimensions prevent construction errors
  • Saves time – Readable drawings don't need verbal explanation

7. Disadvantages of Poor Lettering

  • Drawing may be rejected – Illegible text fails WAEC/NECO
  • Causes expensive mistakes – Misreading "6" as "0" ruins manufacturing
  • Looks unprofessional – Reflects badly on the drafter
  • Wastes time – Others must ask for clarification
Practical Assignment:
1. On A4 paper, draw guidelines and write the alphabet A-Z in 5mm vertical single-stroke capitals.
2. Write numbers 0-9 in 3.5mm vertical single-stroke.
3. Letter the following in 5mm: "TECHNICAL DRAWING" and "SS1 FIRST TERM"
4. State 4 rules for good lettering.
